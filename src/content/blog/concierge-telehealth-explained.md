The word "concierge" gets attached to a lot of things in healthcare. Some of them deserve it. Some don't. The word started as a way to describe a small set of medical practices that took fewer patients per provider, charged a membership fee, and offered direct access to a doctor in exchange. It was specific. It meant something.

It still means something, but the term has been borrowed by direct-to-consumer telehealth companies, med-spas, and various subscription services that have very little to do with the original idea. If you're trying to figure out whether a "concierge" service is actually delivering concierge care, here's what to look for.

## The original definition

Concierge medicine emerged in the late 1990s as a response to the patient-volume pressure on primary care. The economics of insurance-based primary care had pushed average appointment lengths down to seven minutes. Doctors were seeing 25-35 patients a day. Real conversations weren't possible.

A small number of physicians started offering an alternative: significantly fewer patients (typically 200-600 per provider, versus 2,000-3,000 in standard practice), longer appointments, direct phone or text access, and an annual or monthly membership fee that funded the smaller panel.

The economic logic was straightforward. Fewer patients per provider means more time per patient. More time per patient means better diagnostic depth, better treatment plans, and better outcomes. The membership model funds the lower volume.

That's the core of concierge medicine. Everything else is decoration.

## What it should actually deliver

A concierge practice that's earning the term has four characteristics:

**Direct provider access.** When you have a question, you message the provider. Not a triage nurse, not a chatbot, not a generic support inbox. The provider who knows you. Response times measured in hours, not days.

**Time per appointment that allows real conversation.** Forty-five-minute first visits are typical. Twenty-to-thirty-minute follow-ups. Compare this to seven-minute appointments in volume-based practice and you start to understand why concierge gets results that seem disproportionate to the medication or procedure being delivered.

**Continuity.** Same provider every time. Your file, your history, your trajectory. The provider remembers what you tried last month and what didn't work in 2023. This is rare in any kind of medicine and almost impossible in DTC platforms where you talk to whoever is on shift.

**A protocol, not just a transaction.** The medication or procedure is part of a larger plan that includes monitoring, adjustment, adjacent therapies, and a clear path through plateaus and complications. The provider is invested in the outcome, not just the next refill.

If a service is missing one of these, it's something else. It might still be valuable. But it isn't concierge.

## Where direct-to-consumer telehealth fits

DTC telehealth platforms (Hims, Ro, Henry Meds, Sesame, and similar) are not concierge, even when they use the word. They're pharmacy companies with a brief medical workflow attached to satisfy state prescribing requirements.

This isn't a criticism. They serve a real purpose. For a healthy adult who knows what they want and has a simple presenting issue, DTC telehealth is faster, cheaper, and more convenient than traditional primary care. They've expanded access to medications that used to require multiple in-person visits and significant cost.

But the model has structural limits. Provider time per patient is measured in minutes. Patient-provider continuity is incidental. The clinical workflow is optimized for prescribing speed, not for problem-solving. When something goes wrong (a side effect, an unexpected lab result, a plateau), the model is poorly equipped to respond.

If your situation is simple and you're confident you know what you need, DTC works fine. If your situation is complex, or you're managing a long-term protocol, or you want a provider who understands you as a person rather than a chart, DTC will frustrate you within months.

## What concierge telehealth specifically delivers

Concierge telehealth is the marriage of two ideas: the lower-volume relationship-based model of traditional concierge, applied through a 100% virtual care delivery method.

The "telehealth" half is the convenience layer. You're not driving to an office, sitting in a waiting room, or scheduling around the provider's calendar. Visits happen on video from wherever you are. Labs are drawn at a Quest or LabCorp near you, results sync back electronically. Medications ship to your door.

The "concierge" half is the relationship layer. Your provider is a specific person. They know you. You message them, not a portal queue. The protocol is built around your goals, your history, your training, your life.

The combination is genuinely new. It removes the geographic limits of traditional concierge (you don't have to live in the same city as your provider) and the volume pressure of standard telehealth (your provider isn't running a queue of fifty patients a day). For a certain kind of patient, it's the most efficient model in healthcare today.

## What it costs and what you're paying for

Concierge telehealth pricing for performance medicine, hormone optimization, or weight management typically ranges from $200 to $700 per month, all-in. Lower tiers cover simpler protocols (basic HRT, micro-dose metabolic). Higher tiers cover more complex multi-medication protocols (recomposition, peak performance, regenerative medicine).

Compared to a traditional concierge primary care membership ($150-$300 monthly fee, plus all the actual medical care still goes through insurance), concierge telehealth is actually a similar number. The difference is that the medication and supplies are usually included, where in concierge primary care they're billed separately to insurance.

What you're paying for, in a properly designed concierge telehealth practice:

The medication and supplies (the actual product cost is a meaningful but not enormous percentage of the total).

Provider time. Real time. Forty-five minutes for a first visit. Direct messaging access between visits. Quarterly check-ins built into the cadence rather than billed separately.

Lab work. Baseline panels and periodic re-checks, included in the membership rather than billed.

Coordination. The provider managing the moving pieces, calling the pharmacy when needed, adjusting the protocol when something's off, keeping the picture coherent over months and years.

If you compare the per-month cost to the per-minute cost of getting equivalent provider time and coordination through traditional channels, concierge telehealth is usually less expensive. It just bills differently.

## When concierge telehealth is the right fit

It's the right fit if you want:

- Direct relationship with a specific provider over months and years, not a different person every time
- A protocol that adjusts to you, not a standard regimen pushed onto everyone
- Coordination across labs, medications, and adjacent therapies rather than fragmented care
- Convenience of telehealth without losing the clinical depth of a real medical relationship

It's not the right fit if you:

- Just need a quick prescription for an uncomplicated indication and don't want a relationship
- Already have a primary care doctor you love and just need a specialist for one thing
- Are in a complex medical situation that requires in-person specialty care (some endocrine, oncologic, or cardiac scenarios)
