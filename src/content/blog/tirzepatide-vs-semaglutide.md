Patients ask this question almost every week. The honest answer is: both work. The differences are smaller than the marketing suggests, and the choice depends more on your specific situation than on which medication is "better."

Here's what's actually true.

## What each medication is

**Semaglutide** is a GLP-1 receptor agonist. It's the active ingredient in Ozempic (the diabetes brand), Wegovy (the weight-loss brand), and most compounded semaglutide. It mimics a hormone your gut produces after eating, which slows gastric emptying, increases satiety, and reduces food noise. It's been on the market since 2017.

**Tirzepatide** is a dual agonist. It activates both the GLP-1 receptor and the GIP receptor (a related gut hormone). It's the active ingredient in Mounjaro (diabetes), Zepbound (weight loss), and most compounded tirzepatide. It's been on the market since 2022.

The dual-agonist mechanism in tirzepatide is the source of most of the practical differences. GIP appears to amplify GLP-1's effects on appetite and may have additional metabolic benefits, including better preservation of lean mass during weight loss for some patients.

## The clinical evidence

Both medications have been studied at scale.

In semaglutide's pivotal weight-loss trial (STEP-1), patients without diabetes who took 2.4mg weekly lost an average of fifteen percent of their body weight over 68 weeks. That's a substantial result. The placebo group lost about two percent.

In tirzepatide's weight-loss trial (SURMOUNT-1), patients without diabetes who took the high dose (15mg) lost an average of twenty-one percent of their body weight over 72 weeks. The mid-dose (10mg) produced about nineteen percent loss. The placebo group lost about three percent.

Head-to-head data is limited but consistent: in trials directly comparing the two, tirzepatide produces faster onset of effect and slightly greater total weight loss, on average. Both produce real, durable results when taken correctly.

The gap is not as large as some patients believe based on social media. A motivated patient on semaglutide will often outperform a less-engaged patient on tirzepatide. The protocol around the medication matters as much as the medication itself.

## The side effect profiles

Both medications share the same general side effect profile, since both work through similar mechanisms.

The most common side effects are gastrointestinal: nausea, occasional vomiting, constipation, diarrhea, and reduced appetite to a degree that can feel uncomfortable in the first few weeks. These almost always fade as your body adjusts, typically within two to four weeks of starting or stepping up a dose.

Less common but worth knowing: gallbladder issues, particularly during rapid weight loss. Rare but documented: pancreatitis. Both medications have a black-box warning about thyroid C-cell tumors based on rodent studies, though the human signal is unclear and contested. Patients with a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN-2 should not take either medication.

In direct comparisons, tirzepatide's side effects appear marginally more pronounced at higher doses, which makes sense given its stronger appetite-suppression effect. But the difference is small and very dose-dependent. Patients who titrate up slowly tolerate either medication well.

## The dose ladders

Semaglutide for weight loss typically starts at 0.25mg weekly, escalating monthly through 0.5mg, 1.0mg, 1.7mg, and finally 2.4mg. Most patients reach a meaningful effect at 1.0mg and a strong effect at 1.7-2.4mg.

Tirzepatide starts at 2.5mg weekly, escalating monthly through 5mg, 7.5mg, 10mg, 12.5mg, and 15mg. Many patients reach a strong effect at 7.5mg or 10mg and never need to go higher.

The escalation timeline matters. Patients who try to climb the dose ladder too fast experience more side effects than necessary. Patients who climb too slowly leave benefit on the table. Both medications reward thoughtful titration.

## When tirzepatide is the better fit

Tirzepatide is generally a better fit if:

- You have significant weight to lose (more than 50 pounds) and want the most effective tool available
- You have insulin resistance or pre-diabetes, where the dual-agonist mechanism may help more
- You've tried semaglutide and either plateaued or didn't respond strongly
- You want the most aggressive single-agent weight-loss option

## When semaglutide is the better fit

Semaglutide is generally a better fit if:

- You're fine-tuning weight or have less to lose (10-30 pounds)
- You're on a tighter monthly budget (compounded semaglutide is typically cheaper than compounded tirzepatide)
- You've had GI side effects on other medications and want a slower, gentler ramp
- Your insurance covers brand-name semaglutide for a covered indication

## When the answer is "neither" or "both"

GLP-1 medications are powerful tools but they aren't right for everyone. Patients with active eating disorders, severe gastroparesis, certain thyroid conditions, or some pancreatic histories may not be candidates. Pregnancy is an absolute contraindication.

Some patients also benefit from sequential or combined protocols: starting with semaglutide, switching to tirzepatide for a plateau, or eventually moving to maintenance dosing. If your doctor put you on either medication and you're losing weight, you're on the right one. The differences between the two are real but smaller than most people think. Side effect profiles overlap heavily. Both work for most patients.

If you're choosing from scratch and have flexibility, tirzepatide tends to produce better results for most patients with significant weight to lose, but semaglutide remains an excellent choice and is often more accessible.

The medication is a tool. The protocol around it (lab work, titration, support, training, sleep, protein) is what actually delivers durable results.
