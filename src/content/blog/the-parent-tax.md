I'm a nurse practitioner. I'm also a mom. So I know the specific arithmetic of putting yourself last — the appointment you cancel because someone spiked a fever, the workout that quietly became a load of laundry, the five broken hours of sleep you call "fine" because you don't have a better option.

Here is the thing I have to tell patients, and remind myself: the body keeps a ledger. You can defer your own care, but you can't make the bill disappear. It just moves to a different column — your metabolism, your hormones, your bloodwork.

This isn't a guilt piece. It's a clinical one. Let me show you what the evidence says is actually happening when a parent runs on empty for years, and what's worth doing about it.

## Self-neglect isn't a character flaw — it's a pattern

Parents and caregivers systematically deprioritize their own health. This is well documented in the caregiver-burden literature and in the newer research on **parental burnout** — a distinct state of exhaustion that builds when the demands of parenting outrun the resources to meet them. It is not weakness and it is not just you. It's structural, and it's common.

Naming it matters, because "I just need to push through" is the exact instinct that turns a hard season into a measurable health problem.

## Short sleep is a metabolic event, not just tiredness

If I could fix one thing for most depleted parents, it would be sleep — because it's the lever with the most evidence behind it.

Chronic short sleep is not only feeling foggy. In controlled research it measurably impairs glucose metabolism and insulin sensitivity, and it shifts your appetite hormones — leptin (fullness) down, ghrelin (hunger) up — toward eating more and craving denser, sweeter food. Over time, short sleep is associated with weight gain and cardiometabolic risk.

So the parent who is "doing everything right" with food and still gaining weight, exhausted, reaching for sugar at 3 p.m. — that's not a willpower story. That's physiology responding exactly as it's supposed to, to too little sleep.

## Chronic stress has a hormonal signature

Sustained stress keeps cortisol elevated when it should be falling, and cortisol interacts with blood sugar, with where your body stores fat (abdominal especially), with sleep, and with mood. Chronically, that pattern is linked to cardiometabolic risk.

For women in their late thirties and forties, this often lands at the same time as **perimenopause** — so you can have two depleting forces stacked on each other, each amplifying the other's symptoms. (I wrote separately about [why perimenopause starts earlier than most women are told](/blog/perimenopause-starts-earlier).) When a patient is fighting both at once and being told "your labs are normal," no wonder she feels unseen.

## "Put your own mask on first" is biology, not a slogan

The airplane line is a cliché because it's true at the level of physiology. Your health is the infrastructure your family runs on. And the research backs the reframe: parental wellbeing is consistently linked to child wellbeing. Taking care of yourself isn't taking something *from* your kids — for the long game, it's one of the better things you can do *for* them.

You are allowed to be a patient, not just a caregiver.

## What actually moves the needle for busy parents

Realistic beats perfect. Here's the evidence-based short list, in priority order:

- **Protect sleep first — even imperfectly.** It's the highest-return lever and the first one parents lose. Guarding it is medicine.
- **Protein and resistance training.** They preserve the muscle and metabolic health that stress and aging chip away at. Small and frequent beats heroic and unsustainable.
- **Get the labs instead of guessing.** Thyroid, metabolic markers, and — when the picture fits — hormones. Don't normalize symptoms that have a findable, treatable cause.
- **Treat what's treatable.** "Tired and heavy and anxious" is not a personality you're stuck with; it's often a set of inputs that can change.
- **The smallest sustainable change wins.** The perfect protocol you can't keep loses to the modest one you actually do.
